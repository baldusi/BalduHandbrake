### 1. The Home Base: GitHub & Maker Platforms
Before you post on social channels, your files need a bulletproof home. Do not underestimate the power of presentation here.
* **GitHub:** This is where the code, KiCAD files, and DWGs should live. 
    * *The README is everything:* Include a high-quality GIF of the OLED menu in action right at the top. List the BOM (Bill of Materials) clearly. Link the YouTube videos directly in the documentation.
* **Printables / MakerWorld:** If there are any 3D-printed components (housings for the OLED/ESP32, pommel mounts), host them here. These platforms have massive, search-friendly DIY sim racing tags. Link back to your GitHub for the software and PCB files.
* **Hackaday.io:** Create a project page here. Hackaday’s community *loves* ESP32 hardware integrations, custom PCBs, and hydraulic engineering. Once documented, submit it to their **Tip Line**. If the editors write an article about your handbrake, it will instantly validate the project for thousands of engineers.

### 2. The Core Sim Racing DIY Communities (Where the builders are)
These are the spaces where people actually build hardware and where boutique manufacturers lurk for inspiration.

* **Discord Communities (The most active DIY scenes):**
    * **Turn Racing Discord:** Turn Racing has a massive DIY community channel. It is filled with people making custom steering wheels, button boxes, and pedals. Dropping your project here will get immediate, high-level technical attention.
    * **Lebois Racing Discord:** Lebois is famous for their open-source 3D-printed load-cell pedals and H-pattern shifters. Their community is exactly your target audience: hardcore sim racers who love open-source, high-end DIY gear.
* **Dedicated Forums:**
    * **OverTake.gg (Formerly RaceDepartment):** Their "Sim Racing Hardware - DIY" subforum is legendary. Create a comprehensive, well-formatted thread detailing your build process, the specs, and links to your files.
    * **xSimulator.net:** While heavily focused on motion rigs, this community is filled with electronics nerds who build high-power simulator equipment. They will appreciate the transducer and ADC integration.
* **Reddit:**
    * Post to **`r/simracing`** using the "DIY" flair. 
    * *Crucial tip for Reddit:* Do not just post a text link to GitHub. Post a 30-to-60-second video showing you ripping the handbrake in a sim (like *Dirt Rally 2.0* or *Assetto Corsa*), cutting to a close-up of the OLED screen changing the brake curve. Put the links and explanation in the top comment.
